Controversies

Learn more about the many different controversies surrounding employee monitoring.

Introduction

Although surveillance in the workplace is quite common, especially due to the rise of at home workers due to the COVID-19 pandemic, that does not mean it is excluded from having a variety of opinions based around it and how it should be carried out. Make sure to check out the Employer Considerations page to learn more about some reasons employers may implement workplace surveillance.

There are three main scenarios that become controversial when it comes to employee monitoring: where monitoring should begin and end, where that information is required, and where the monitoring impacts the effectiveness of employees and the trust between employers and employees. However, monitoring employees can sometimes improve productivity, protect the company secrets and provide evidence to protect companies against legal liability.

Benificial Aspects

There are quite a few benefits for employers in the sense of employee monitoring. For example, the business may benefit by being better able to prevent theft, protection of intellectual property, improved decision-making for managers and other leaders, and aide in protecting against legal liability through providing evidence in various legal disputes.

Workers can also benefit from being monitored by their employers. When it is used appropriately, surveillance may protect employees from multiple types of false accusations. Some employees may even use surveillance capabilities to obtain information on their employers to expose violations and facilitate collective bargaining.

Negative Aspects

However, there are also some key drawbacks regarding employee monitoring especially when it comes to its impacts on employees' privacy, its impacts on the trust relationship between employees and employers, and the incentives it creates. When used inappropriately, employee surveillance can be perceived as an infringement on privacy, specifically when employees did not previously consent to the collection of their data. This in turn causes major amounts of mutual trust and confidence lost between the employer and their employee.

Other Considerations

There have even been studies regarding employee monitoring and the health effects it has on employees. Some workers have reported higher levels of stress and low morale due to the pressure to perform. This can eventually cause attrition of good employees. Morally speaking, the right to privacy should not be violated so easily without strong justification, but there is still the problem of power imbalance. This imbalance between employers and employees creates an economic coercion that may force employees to consent to surveillance to retain their positions. Overt surveillance has sometimes led to resistance from workers. This can take multiple forms. For example, subverting surveillance measures through leaving phone lines open for long periods of time after they are no longer being used.

In Conclusion

Luckily, there have been multiple federal protections against workplace surveillance. However, these are limited and ineffectual. The Electronic Communications Privacy Act of 1986, for example, has a business exception allowing employers to monitor communications for legitimate business reasons, and the "consent exception" allowing monitoring of communications where employees consent. This has allowed plenty of room for businesses to maneuver around the rules to excessively monitor their employees. States have also pushed for various protection laws, such as the California Consumer Privacy Act of 2018, Colorado's SB 21-190, and the Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act. All these protection laws are being enacted to ensure that data that is collected by businesses on individuals must be disclosed if requested. This also includes their own employees. However, disclosure of information alone is not sufficient enough to protect against overzealous surveillance.

Ultimately, the best solution would be collective bargaining between employers and employees. This can be used as a tool to create worker-friendly protections that may counteract detrimental impacts of surveillance in the workplace. Therefore, many legislators and regulators should seek to strengthen collective bargaining rights. This can be done by facilitating unionization to allow the unions to mandate negotiations for workers and increasing both the employees' and unions' awareness of their rights to negotiate surveillance policies.
